Heat Exchanger Tube Rupture

Heat exchanger tube rupture occurs when an internal failure in a heat exchanger allows high-pressure fluid to flow into the lower-pressure side, potentially causing overpressure of the low-pressure side and connected equipment.

This scenario must be evaluated for any heat exchanger where the high-pressure side exceeds the low-pressure side's design pressure. Common causes include tube corrosion, erosion, vibration-induced fatigue, thermal cycling, and brittle fracture.

API-521 Section 4.4.14: Heat Transfer Equipment Failure

Per API-521 Section 4.4.14, key considerations include:

  • Pressure Criteria: Relief not required if low-pressure side design pressure exceeds high-pressure side operating pressure, or if overpressure would not exceed corrected hydrotest pressure
  • Design Basis: Typically assumes complete rupture of one tube (guillotine break at tubesheet)
  • Alternative Analysis: Detailed analysis considering tube material, thickness, corrosion rates, and inspection programs may justify smaller leak size
  • Exchanger Types: Different considerations for shell-and-tube, double-pipe, and plate-and-frame exchangers
  • Fluid Considerations: Must account for flashing liquids, two-phase flow, and intimate mixing effects
  • Dynamic Analysis: Recommended for large pressure differentials (>1000 psi) or liquid-filled low-pressure sides
  • Device Location: PRD should be located directly on exchanger or immediately adjacent to minimize pressure transients

Calculation Method

For Liquids (Non-flashing): Use incompressible flow through orifice equations. Flow = C × A × √(2gΔP/ρ) where C is discharge coefficient (~0.6 for sharp-edged orifice).

For Gases: Use compressible flow equations accounting for choked vs non-choked conditions based on critical pressure ratio.

For Flashing Liquids: Two-phase flow methods (HEM model) should be used. This tool provides simplified calculation; detailed analysis may be required.

Note: This tool provides steady-state calculations. For high pressure differentials or liquid-filled systems, dynamic analysis is recommended per API-521 Section 4.4.14.2.2.

Exchanger Configuration

Exchanger Types:
Shell-and-Tube: Most common. Full tube rupture assumed unless detailed analysis justifies smaller leak.
Double-Pipe: Schedule pipe less likely to rupture. Weld failures possible with dissimilar metals.
Plate-and-Frame: More likely to leak at external gaskets. Internal plate failures can occur from corrosion.
Fluid State on High-Pressure Side:
Liquid: Non-flashing liquid (stays liquid at low-pressure side conditions)
Gas: Vapor or gas on high-pressure side
Flashing Liquid: Liquid that flashes to vapor when pressure drops (two-phase flow)
For volatile fluids being heated, consider flashing effects.
