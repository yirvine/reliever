Blocked Outlet (Closed Outlet)

Blocked outlet (also called closed outlet) occurs when an outlet valve is inadvertently closed while equipment is on stream, exposing the equipment to pressure that can exceed its MAWP. This scenario must be evaluated for any valve (manual, control, or remotely operated) that could be inadvertently closed during operation.

Common causes include operator error closing manual valves, control valve failure in the closed position, or inadvertent closure of remotely operated valves during maintenance or switchovers. The scenario is particularly critical for equipment downstream of pumps or pressure sources.

API-521 Section 4.4.2: Closed Outlets

Per API-521 Section 4.4.2, key considerations include:

  • Design to Source Pressure: If equipment is designed to the maximum source pressure, closure of an outlet valve will not result in overpressure, so a PRD is not required
  • Centrifugal Pumps: System does not require relief if pump, piping, and equipment are designed to withstand maximum shut-in pressure of the pump
  • Positive Displacement Pumps: Pressure-relief protection is usually required to protect the pump itself and downstream equipment against shut-in conditions
  • Administrative Controls: Can be used for manual valves unless resulting pressure exceeds corrected hydrotest pressure (see API-521 Section 4.2.2)
  • Outlet Flow Credit: Credit can be taken for valves that remain in normal operating position during the failure (per API-521 Section 4.4.2.4)

The required relieving rate should be determined at relieving conditions (not normal operating conditions). The effect of frictional pressure drop in connecting lines should also be considered when determining the required relieving rate.

Calculation Method

The relieving rate for blocked outlet is typically the maximum flow rate from the pressure source (pump or upstream system) at relieving conditions. For centrifugal pumps, this is the maximum flow at the pump's shut-in head. For positive displacement pumps, this is the pump's maximum capacity. For pressure-driven sources, use the maximum flow that can be delivered at the source pressure.

Note: The ASME VIII design flow includes a 1/0.9 multiplier per ASME Section VIII requirements for liquid relief sizing, accounting for the 110% accumulation allowance.

Other: Other pressure sources requiring evaluation
Maximum pressure that the source can deliver:
Centrifugal Pump: Shut-in head at dead-head conditions
Positive Displacement Pump: Maximum discharge pressure capability
Pressure Source: Maximum pressure of upstream vessel or header
If this pressure does not exceed vessel MAWP and equipment is designed for it, relief may not be required.
Maximum flow rate from the source at relieving conditions:
Centrifugal Pump: Flow at shut-in head (typically near zero for high-head pumps)
Positive Displacement Pump: Maximum pump capacity (displacement × speed)
Pressure Source: Maximum flow based on pressure differential and line sizing
API-521 Section 4.4.2.4: Outlet Flow Credit

Per API-521, credit can be taken for valves that remain in their normal operating position during the blocked outlet scenario.

Credit can be taken for other outlet valves (manual, control, or pump discharge) that are not affected by the primary failure and remain operational.

Example: A vessel receives 10,000 lb/hr from a pump. One outlet valve is closed (blocked), but another independent outlet normally discharges 2,000 lb/hr. If this second outlet remains operational, the relief device only needs to handle the net: 8,000 lb/hr.

Calculation: Net Relief Flow = Maximum Inlet Flow - Normal Outlet Flow
